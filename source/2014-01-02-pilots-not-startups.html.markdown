---
title:  "Make pilots, not startups"
date:   2014-01-02 18:12:00
---

My approach to making software has changed.

I used to look for the big startup idea.  An idea big enough to build a company and raise money around.  An idea that was worth at least 3 years of my life.

I don't do that anymore.

I found that no single idea was worth that much of my time.  I fundamentally like to make new things.  That is how I learn and grow.

I also found that the process of launching a startup is flawed.  It goes against all of the things I've learned about the process of engineering software.  There is too much pressure placed on the initial release.  As a result of being stealth, most startup launches are essentially done in a Big-Bang fashion.  Everything is riding on the launch.  Not only does the software have to function correctly but it also has to be something that users want and meet their expectations.  The window of opportunity is small and the time to fix and iterate is usually not large enough.

There is too much ego woven into a startup.  For many, making an untested idea work takes over their lives.  It becomes an elaborate demonstration to people in their lives that they *are* in fact *successful*.  At some point the founders end up clutching to an idea that won't work because they are in denial.  If the startup is lucky they have enough time to pivot but by that point the whole venture has turned from building something great into an operation to save face.

Startups are naturally stabs in the dark as to what users want.  An original idea can only be validated once it's launched.  The problem: it's really hard to guess what users will want that they've never thought they needed before now.  You can have the perfect investors, a perfect team and perfect code and you can still easily build something that no one wants.

TV has a similar problem.  Networks field a bunch of new show ideas and produce single episode pilots.  From that bunch a few of those pilots will actually make it to air.  From those that make it to air maybe 1 or 2 tend to actually stick around beyond a first season.

It's hard to know what people will like so let's just embrace that uncertainty.

That's why I'm building pilots instead of startups.

I'm focusing on ideas that I can build as an individual without a team and release them early and often.  Servers are so cheap to run and scale that the only real cost at this point is time.  For many smaller scale ideas investment is simply not required.

Swipe Stopper and Cloudgram are pilots.  I'm already working on the next pilot (FANS.FM) that will launch this month.  Beyond that I have a few more ideas bouncing around.

My goal is to release these ideas as a starting point and refine them based on what users are actually looking for.  By removing the pressure and infrastructure of the startup mindset these pilots can have the necessary room to breathe and improve.  

Most of these pilots won't ultimately succeed and that is ok.  I learn so much from building each one that it's always worth it.
